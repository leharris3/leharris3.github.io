## I. Introduction
Biology is the **study of life**. It is an exploration of the structure and function of all living things. Life, and Biology in general, bares a number of similarities to the world of Computer Science. That is, upon closer inspection, life appears to work a lot like a computer. In order for living to function, they must break up complicated tasks into a number of well-defined sub-tasks. In the same way that your computer (the one you reading this document on now) divides functionality into different components (e.g., CPU, RAM, etc), so to do living things employ a sort compartmentalization. 

We divide Biology into **five major themes** to make it a little more palletable.

    1. Evolution
    2. Flow of Information: This includes the exhange of genetic material between organisms.
    3. Structure and Function: Simply, what do components of life look like and what do they do?
    4. Transformation of Matter and Energy: Life is matter fueled by energy, as a result the interactions between these two concepts become important.
    5. Interactions Between Systems: Here we refer to biological systems big and small. These can be ecoystems or hormonal systems.

So we can say that life follows a kind of hierarchy. Livings things and their components can be organized from small to big to stuff in a meaningful way. It is our goal in this exploration to develop a top-to-bottom understanding of the basics of biology. This is difficult. Biology is a big field, and spans a near endless number of topics. Therefore, as we work our way up the scaled of life, we may be forced to take the occasional detour. This is necessiary so that we don't leave out vital Biological processes that may be to needed later.

Notice that we try our best to group concepts by relative scale. Before we jump into cells, however, it is necessary to provide a good definition of life. What makes a thing a "living thing" anyways? We say that a thing is "alive" if it meets a special set of critera (or rules). There is no *official* set of rules, but generally biologists agree that living must be able to respire, grow, excrete, reproduce, matabolize, move, and be resposive to the enviornment[1]. This means that things like our friend the Amoeba are considered living, but viruses for example don't make the cut. As a result, any hunk of matter not conforming to these critera are discluded from discussions of Biology.

## II. Cells

Cells are the smallest unit of life. Crazily cells are both **alive** and **make up living things**. This means that big, complicated living things like yourself are made up of many tiny, smaller living things. Neat! But before we can dive into cells, we need to talk about the non-living stuff that makes up cells.

### A. Organic Molecules
Organic molecules are the building blocks of cells. We define an organic molecule to be any chemical compound (bunch of atoms bound together) in which a **carbon atom is bound to a hydrogen atom**. These arrangements of atoms give cells their structure, and are a vital component of life on Earth.
